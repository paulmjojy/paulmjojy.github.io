Abstract¶

Every year, hundreds of NBA players compete for one of the sport's most coveted awards. The NBA Most Valuable Player award. The MVP is given to the one player who contributed the most to their team's success during the regular season. The recipient of this prestigious honor is selected by a committee, who makes the selection based on a number of both objective and subjective factors.

Some of the objective factors that go into the decision process include:

  • Points Per Game
  • Rebounds Per Game
  • Assists Per Game
  • Team Wins Some of the Subjective factors include:
  • Popularity with Owners
  • Popularity with Fans

For more information on the selection process, see here: https://bleacherreport.com/articles/2703746-how-the-nba-mvp-voting-process-works-announcement-date

As you can see, it is difficult to tell who the committee will select, because there are subjective factors that go into selecting the MVP. But what if we could create an algorithm that would predict the next MVP?

Creating the algorithm¶

We wanted to create an algorithm that would take into account points, rebounds, steals, turnovers, and assists. However, we wanted also wanted to account for shots made vs. shots missed, because missing a shot hurts the team, making the player less valuable. So, we came up with the formula:

$(2PtFGM*2) - (2PtFGA*.XX) + (3PtFGM*3) - (3PtFGA*.XX) + (FTM) - (FTA*-.XX) + Rebounds + Assists + Blocks + Steals - Turnovers$

Where

  • FGM = Field Goals Made
  • FGA = Field Goals Attempted
  • FTM = Free Throws Made
  • FTA = Free Throws Attempted

We also added multipliers to FGM and FGA. 2 point shots made are multiplied by 2, whereas 3 point shots made are multiplied by 3, because they are worth more points and therefore are more valuable to the team.

There's also a fractional multiplier (.XX) next to the shot attempts. These fractions represent the league average shot percentage for that type of shot. These will be calculated once we have organized the data set. If the league average percentage is higher, then a miss will be penalized more. If the league average is lower, then a miss will be penalized less.

A missed free throw has a negative multiplier because the fact that a player gets to the free throw line is valuable, even if they miss; this is because When a player gets to the free throw line, it is usually because he was fouled. A foul is detrimental to the opposing team and therefore beneficial to the player's team. Furthermore, the game clock is stopped and teammates have a chance to rest and gather themselves, making it a valuable asset, especially in close games.

Turnovers are subtracted because turnovers are detrimental to a team and therefore hurts a player's value to that team.

2. Data Processing¶

In the 2nd step of the data lifecycle, we will be tidying our data. The data is not perfect; there will be missing fields, extranneous fields, etc. We as data scientists need to know what to do when we encounter data that does not exactly match our needs. That is where tidying data comes in. Data tidying is the process in which we will modify our data to match our needs. This will include actions such as deleting missing fields, removing extra columns, joining tables, etc.

First, we will trim our MVP Candidates Table to only include the top 5 candidates from each year. Next, we will drop all the uneccessary columns from our Players dataframe. We will achieve this by using the 'drop' function in pandas.

Then, We converted all the columns to floats in order to make calculations easier. We calculated the shooting averages for the whole league in order to apply the multipliers to our formula. We will add a new column to this year's NBA Players dataframe, which we named MVP Calc. This is where we will store the calculations from our algorithm.

Then, we will use our database of NBA Players to find the stats for those candidates, and join them together. Afterwards, we will add a new column to both our MVP Candidates dataframe and our 2018 NBA Players Dataframe. This column would contain our MVP algorithm for each player.

4 Machine Learning¶

As mentioned beforehand, we wanted to see if the algorithm present would be able to predict the next MVP for this NBA season. You would figure that the NBA player who scores the most points or gets the most rebounds, steals, blocks, etc, would be the one to win it. Unfortunately, the MVP isn't solely judged on performance, because if it were, there would be no point in determining or predicting the next MVP. In order to show how accurate the MVP algorithm is, we wanted to compare our "MVP index", but given the trouble to show how ACCURATE our algorithm would be, with a linear regression model, we went and did some research on what could possibly the best metric out there to compare with. We discovered a metric that not only has had success in determining the MVP, but also has been able able to accurately identify 50%, or more, of the top 5 voted players for MVP, EACH YEAR since 1996, at the NBA awards ceremony.

The Player Impact Estimate (PIE)¶

As the name may hint to, the Player Impact Estimate (PIE) measures how much impact a player has on each game. Unlike our algorithm, the NBA somehow was able to come up with an algorithm that also took into account the statistical biases that are apparent in the league. In order to prove how accurate our algorithm is, we want to run our "MVP index" with the PIE index on the current 2018-2019 season, and see how similar our algorithm is to theres. One thing that was found to be defective given the PIE index, was the occasional situations where people who had just played just a few games had the same PIE scores as phenomenal players such as Lebron, Stephen, Durant, etc. And this kicked off people from the top 5 PIE scores for the season, whom were occassionally the top 5 MVP candidates. Along with that, despite PIE scores measuring for bias, years such as from 1997-1998, when the all-great Michael Jordan played, the likes of Jordan had PIE rankings BELOW 10th place, and despite haveing such a low PIE score during that season, he ended up winning the MVP award.

Final Thoughts¶

We see that there is a relation between our algorithm and stat leaders through the various categories (points, rebounds, etc). So we can assume our algorithm is fairly accurate.

This study is important because the MVP race is hotly contested and highly subjective. Removing some of the subjective bias would remove some of the mystery surrounding the potential winner.
